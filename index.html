Blacknose shark

Carcharhinus acronotus

A type of requiem shark found in tropical Atlantic waters.

Gives live birth (litter size 1-6) in nursery habitats, often near mangroves or coastal bays.

The black dot is less apparent in older individuals.

Life span estimated at 15-19 years, reaching maturity ~4.5 years.

Assessed as Endangered in 2009.

Green moray eel

Gymnothorax funebris

This moray is actually brown! A thick layer of yellow mucus gives it its green hue.

Nocturnal and solitary, it hunts in crevices of reefs.

If prey appears too large, the eels can “tie” it up with their bodies and tear it into smaller bites.

Known for their moveable pharyngeal jaws (think the Xenomorph queen from Alien). This allows them to swallow prey without stretching their mouth wider than the caverns they hunt in.

Whitespotted filefish

Cantherhines macrocerus

Like triggerfish, filefish have a distinct dorsal spine, which they use for protection—they erect it after swimming into crevices in the reef, thereby wedging their bodies inside the space.

Their bodies are often also covered in setae (small spines) that give their skin a sandpaper-like texture.

Males usually have longer spines on the caudal peduncle than females.

Blue tang

Acanthurus coeruleus

The Atlantic blue tang is a type of surgeonfish, recognized by its oval body and noticeable spines (like scalpels) near the caudal peduncle.

It has distinct color phases, often displaying bright yellow as a juvenile with yellow caudal fins; and a purple gray or bright blue body as the fish transitions to its fully blue adult form.

Biofluorescence in larval blue tangs has also been documented, possibly as a form of camouflage, or as a way of communicating with others of their species.

Generally herbivorous — although ours love krill pops!

Doctorfish - juvenile

Doctorfish

Acanthurus chirurgus

Doctorfish are commonly found on the rocky outcroppings of reefs.

Their diet is mostly herbivorous, with their teeth designed to scrape algae from coral and rock.

They swallow food whole, which requires them to have a gizzard-like organ with sand inside to aide in pre-digesting plant matter.

As larvae, they have large dorsal and ventral spines, which help with protection and flotation, to an extent. The scalpel spines usually come in when the fish are ~13 mm.

Atlantic spadefish - juvenile

Atlantic spadefish

Chaetodipterus faber

Like their close relatives the orbicular batfish, Atlantic spadefish are often found in estuaries or coastal waters in their juvenile black form. Juveniles adopt an angular swimming pattern, to resemble dead leaf debris.

Large schools of adults (up to 500) commonly forage and hunt for mollusks, marine worms, crustaceans, and jellies.

Lookdown - juvenile

Lookdown

Selene vomer

Lookdowns are coastal dwelling fish, found in the western Atlantic from Canada to Uruguay.

They hunt for crabs, shrimp, fish, and worms among the hard or sandy substrates of their preferred habitats.

There is little commercial interest in them except for the aquarium trade, where their unique shape and flashy scales make them a sought-after species for display.

Juveniles look festive, with dorsal filaments that they lose by adulthood.

Bar jack

Caranx ruber

A relative of the lookdown, bar jacks are found from New Jersey southwards to the coasts of Venezuela.

They are easily distinguished by the dark horizontal bar that runs along their backs with a bright blue one immediately beneath it.

Spawning happens twice per year with females releasing 67k-230k eggs.

Larvae grow rapidly and the juveniles are often seen near rafts of brown algae or in shallow reefs.

Upon reaching adulthood, they head for deeper offshore waters and more open spaces.

Graysby

Cephalopholis cruentata

These grouper are often seen in seagrass beds or coral reefs from Cape Fear, NC southwards to the Caribbean coasts of South America.

They are identified by their coloration (olive or brown base with red spots) and four contrasting white or black splotches at the base of their dorsal fin.

This species is a protogynous hermaphrodite, where fish start out as female and become male after 4-5 years, immediately after the spawning season.

Black grouper

Mycteroperca bonaci

These large groupers are great ambush predators.

While they lack proper teeth, they have teeth plates in their throats to prevent prey that has been suctioned in from escaping.

Like the Graysby, black groupers are also hermaphrodites, starting life as females with some transitioning to males after 10-14 years. The usual ratio is four females to every male.

They can live ~30 years.

Although a popular commercial and recreational fish, proper management has meant their populations in the Atlantic and Caribbean are stable.

Black durgeon

Melichthys niger

With proper lighting, the black triggerfish/durgeon shows off its lighter colors: dark blue and green, with some orange at the front of its head.

It also has a deep-set groove in front of its eye.

It has a global distribution, populating isolated reefs in tropical latitudes. In the Pacific, it is often confused for its cousin, the Indian, or blackfin, triggerfish.

Hawaiians call it Humuhumu'ele'ele

Queen triggerfish

Balistes vetula

Queen triggerfish are one of the largest and more aggressive triggerfish species; as such they require large exhibits, like ACR.

In the ocean, these fish often prey on sea urchins by blowing them over, thereby gaining access to the urchin’s vulnerable mouth and underside.

Triggerfish in general have a larger telencephalon — a brain region that may help them with spatial awareness and problem solving while hunting.

Sand tilefish

Malacanthus plumieri

Sand tilefish are found in shallow coastal waters (15-500 ft) over sandy substrate. This allows the fish to create a burrow which it can enter when threatened.

They range from Bermuda southwards throughout the Yucatan peninsula, feeding on mantis shrimp, marine worms, and armored chitons.

By undulating its dorsal and anal fins, this species is capable of hovering for extended periods of time, usually near the entrance to their burrows.

Moray eel - larva

The leptocephalus larva of a moray eel.

This larval body type ties tarpons, ladyfish, and eels into the same Order.

Larval tarpon and eels can’t regulate their internal salinity well, and must hatch in water with stable salinities, offshore.

Atlantic tarpon

Megalops atlanticus

Tarpon evolved around 18 million years ago and have remained relatively unchanged since.

Their larval form avoids predation by being transparent, and the larvae do not actually eat, floating along with currents, having minimal energy requirements.

Juveniles can survive in coastal waters with low oxygen, since their swim bladders act as a rudimentary lung, allowing the fish to gulp air at the surface.

Angelfish

There are ~90 different species of angelfish.

Bright and colorful denizens of coral reefs, they are distinguished from butterflyfish by the preopercle spines preceding their gill covering.

While some species are monogamous, others are not. Some form harems, and lekking behavior (competitive displays by multiple males) has been seen in others. With such colorful scales, it’s not hard to see why.

Juveniles may have such different coloration to hide they are potential competitors with adult members of their own species.

Damselfish

These hardy fish are often found in nooks and crannies of rocky or coral reefs. Most are small (unlike their larger Pacific cousin, the Garibaldi).

Longfin damselfish near Belize have been seen farming algae with help of mysid shrimp. The shrimp fertilize the algae with their waste, improving the food quality for the damselfish. Farms with mysid shrimp are defended by damselfish more vigorously than farms without.

Females choose mates based on the quality of a male’s courtship display. Males “signal jump”—swimming up and down quickly while making a pulsing noise. The pulse rate helps females pick.

Hamlet

Hamlets belong to the Family Serranidae, a catch-all that includes seabasses, basslets, hinds, and groupers. Originally, they were all thought to be one species with different color morphs, and there is still debate over how many different species are within their genus.

Some hamlets are known to by synchronous hermaphrodites, meaning they have both male and female reproductive organs. They do not self-fertilize, but mate in pairs with other fish of their same or similar color morph.

They are predatory fish that prey on mysids, shrimp, other small crustaceans, and occasionally other fishes.

Butterflyfish

A group of reef fishes known for diverse mouth and jaw adaptations for selective feeding (ranging from long, forceps-like jaws for pulling prey from crevices, to stubby snouts for rasping on coral).

Most species are known to swim in mated pairs, with some occasionally schooling.

Life expectancy is usually 3-4 years, with maturity being reached after one year.

Since many butterflyfish species rely on coral for food, they can act as an indicator species for the health of a reef.

Wrasse

Wrasses are usually colorful fishes associated with reefs, found in the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian basins.

They are known for their sharp, outward-jutting front teeth, and crushing pharyngeal jaws further back in their throats.

Many wrasses engage in symbiotic cleaning of larger fish (some of whom are natural predators). Their teeth make them excellent candidates for picking off varieties of parasites from the scales and mouths of these fishes. This behavior has also been used in aquaculture, with wrasses keeping parasitic outbreaks among salmon and tilapia in check.

Grunt

Grunts, named for their ability to make noise by grinding their teeth, are often found in salty coastal waters (near reefs or seasonal algae beds) and brackish estuaries.

Many are nocturnal hunters, feeding on benthic invertebrates.

Grunts, among other reef fishes, often thrive in other coastal environments, like mangroves, as juveniles. It has been documented in Belize that reefs located near mangrove forests support 26 times more fish biomass (from juveniles successfully recruiting to the reefs) than reefs without mangroves nearby.

Parrotfish

Parrotfish, like the wrasses, are known for their unique dentition. These teeth are used to scrape off epilithic algae (that is, algae that grows on rocks), and occasionally coral polyps.

Most parrotfish are herbivores, but will ingest benthic invertebrates unlucky enough to be hiding among the algae.

When rocks, coral, or stone pieces are ingested, they are crushed by pharyngeal teeth and excreted as sand. On average, a parrotfish will excrete ~250 g (9 oz) of sand per day.

The humphead parrotfish is estimated to make 90 kg (200 lbs) of sand per year!

Stoplight parrotfish

Sparisoma viride

Named for the marked yellow spot near the pectoral fin, visible only in the terminal phase.

Colors of the stoplight parrotfish in the initial phase, when it could be either a male or a female, are dramatically different from those in the terminal phase, when it is always a male.

Redband parrotfish - initial phase

Redband parrotfish

Sparisoma aurofrenatum

Juveniles are red-brown with two white stripes, and a black blotch behind the upper gill covers.

Initial phase colouration varies greatly.

The body becomes greenish in the terminal phase, with a lighter underside. The tail becomes more square-shaped with black outer tips. An orangish-pink colour band extends from below the eye to the corner of the mouth.
